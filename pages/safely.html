SAFELY

INFORMING PEOPLE ABOUT UNSAFE SITUATIONS WITH REAL-TIME CRIME DATA

Over the course of 20 weeks, our team was able to design and build an iOS application that aims to preemptively inform users of dangerous situations before they enter the physical proximity of danger using real-time data from community-submitted incidents and Seattle Police Department historical crime logs.

MY ROLE

I was responsible for the experience strategy and design of the iOS application. I primarily led our team through the UI design process while assisting with related UX works from January to June 2017.

THE CHALLENGE

Being in a large city, Seattle is home to a lot of crime. Currently, there is no reliable way for someone to know whether an area is safe without going in-person and investigating.

THE APPROACH

We spent the first 10-weeks conducting research through user surveys, producing a competitive analysis, and creating storyboards. Additionally, we created a comprehensive design blueprint consisting of a mood board, wireframes, prototypes, and design specifications. The remaining 10-weeks consisted of user testing and iOS app development.

THE DISCOVERY

WHAT DOES "SAFETY" MEAN TO YOU?

We designed and sent out a Google survey to various family, friends, and coworkers via convenience sampling to gain a better understanding of our users’ safety habits. Some participants were also briefly interviewed afterward to elaborate on some of their responses.


We wanted to know more about how our users perceived their safety living in Seattle and to learn more about what current devices or strategies they used to protect themselves while commuting. After coding and analyzing our results, we came up with the following conclusions that validated and provided insight for some of our initial assumptions:

01

Users feel less safe when walking alone, in the dark, or in unfamiliar areas.

This was an assumption that our team initially made at the start of our project and were not surprised by the nearly unanimous response set we received after asking our users to describe what circumstances they felt less/safer. Several participants also commented feeling safer when they were walking with a male friend while others commented that they consciously made an effort not to use their phone or take it out of their pockets while walking.

02

“Safety” means both physical and mental well-being.

When asked “What does safety mean to you?” we received very similar responses related to the users’ desire to be physically out of harm’s way and also having peace of mind and not having to worry about being harmed, targeted, or assaulted. Users used phrases like “feeling confident, not worried”, “not having to question my personal well-being”, “absence of fear”, and “not having to worry about being harmed”.

03

Young adults DO care about their safety.

38% of the participants felt like Seattle was a relatively safe city in comparison to other cities in the country and about half the participants responded they sometimes walked by themselves and sometimes with other people.

UW Alerts is highly subscribed to, however, many participants felt like it is underutilized and often too vague.

Interviewing our participants about UW Alerts validated some of our previous assumptions about their frustrations with the current system. Many participants mentioned only receiving ‘vague’ statements from UWPD hours or days after incidents, or not receiving alerts at all about “smaller” incidents like assault, robbery, or theft. None of our participants responded hearing about an incident as it was happening, and only 16.4% said they heard about it minutes after. TARGETING OUR USERS

Based on our survey data, we were able to focus on who we are designing for along with who we are not designing for by designating persona types that carefully aligned with our product vision. Originally, our targeted users evolved around commuters who rely on walking or biking as their primary mode of transportation. We soon realized that our product solution may go a step further and target Seattle tourists and new Seattle community members who are looking for a new home within a safe enviornment.

THE APPROACH

Before jumping into prototyping software, we began to dedicate each screen to represent a Safely feature including crowdsourced/SPD incident markers plotted on a map, a search route menu, a timeline feed of nearby incidents, suggested route overview, and en-route display. Because of time constraints, early design considerations that enabled users to get in touch with emergency contacts and authorities were dropped, allowing our developers to focus on geofencing alerts and notifications.

DESIGNING FOR FAMILIARITY

During the early stages of brainstorming and ideation, we spent some time understanding what makes apps like Uber, Snapchat, and Google Maps easy to use and navigate for first time users. We discovered that each of their primary function is presented to users almost immediately after the app opens, giving users an instant call to action. We decided to follow a similar concept by having a map view be the first thing a user sees when the Safely app opens.

DESIGN DIRECTION

The design direction was inspired by Uber Design and its focus to simplify and improve customer lives with impactful design. With a strong desire in making a social impact, we worked hard to make a product that is minimal, simple, and accessible for all by design.



Typography
Avenir Next is used in 4 different weights: Light, Regular, Medium, and Demi-Bold. The typography is implemented to respond to mobile screen sizes that ensure readability and simplicity.



Colors
The dark blue base color represents UI buttons while the red and teal colors emphasize the source of data from either the Seattle Police Department or user-submitted incidents.


Iconography
The purpose of the iconography is to distinguish the type of crime from the user-submitted incident markers plotted on the map.

DATA THAT PROTECTS

PLAN, TRAVEL, AND INFORM SAFELY

CRIME HISTORY

Safely users can quickly view historical data about crime in Seattle that is displayed as a thermal map. Grabbing data from Seattle Police Department's (UWPD included) API gave us the ability to integrate information in a way that gives users a geographic sense of where the most “dangerous” areas are nearby or in neighborhoods of interest.

REAL-TIME INCIDENTS

Safely users can view and interact with real-time community-submitted incidents about unsafe areas that have occurred in close proximity (or distant) prior to leaving for their destination. This simply acts as a high-level neighborhood watch where a single user can make a social impact to the Seattle community.

ROUTE OVERVIEW

Originally, we envisioned the Safely app guiding users to their destinations on a turn-by-turn basis but we needed to think about why users would want to use our navigation system over current leading solutions such as Google Maps. Eventually, user testing results showed that people would much rather use Google and Apple Maps to get to their destination. Our simplified aerial map view gives users the freedom to use their preferred navigational app while Safely becomes more of a precautionary step to take in foreseeing danger before starting a trip.

USERS HELPING OTHERS

Safely users are informed in real-time through push notifications when a crime is posted nearby or in areas along a route. As a primary feature of the Safely app, those who use it can submit an incident to warn others about a dangerous situation or disturbance they have observed or experienced. Submitted incidents are tagged and saved for 72 hours as an interactive marker on the map that specifies the type of crime encountered along with a detailed description.

REPORTING CRIME

Informational interview results gave us reason to rethink our approach on how users inform the Safely community, especially during emergency situations. Originally, we designed Safely to work in a way that gives users the ability to inform others only at the moment an incident occurs. Problem is, that is not always the case. At the time of an emergency situation, users will prioritize their personal safety over the safety of others. So we designed Safely to give users the ability to quickly drop a marker (location of incident) during their route which can then be given more detail once they have safely reached their destination.

EMOTIONAL DESIGN

VISCERAL EMOTIONAL DESIGN

This type of emotional design is what motivated me to take on the role as the UI designer from the start of the project. I wanted to challenge myself to create a quality experience that looked and felt easy to use for first-time users by emphasizing concepts of minimalism. Successfully doing so would highlight the importance of Safely's data and core functionalities.

BEHAVIORAL EMOTIONAL DESIGN

As much as I wanted to design a visually appealing app, I also knew it was important to create a functional user experience. At the core, Safely is designed to protect people from unsafe situations with real-time crime data, meaning that it was important to establish a sense of security during initial interactions. Having that as a key concept allowed me to really evaluate how well and intuitive each functionality performs before transferring my designs over to our developers.

REFLECTIVE EMOTIONAL DESIGN

At the end of the day, reflective emotional design is what matters the most to Safely (and to most digital solutions). Once users find that our product has the potential to make an impact on their lives, we hope it keeps them coming back for more because it holds intrinsic value. Although it was critical to design our Safely app to look appealing and be usable, no value would be given to our product if users did not feel the need to use it for their protection.
