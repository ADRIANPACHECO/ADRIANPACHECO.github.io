Safely: Informing people about unsafe situations with real-time crime data.

Winter 2016 - Spring 2017

Over the course of 20 weeks, team Safely was able to design and build an iOS application that uses map-based visualizations of Seattle Police Department crime logs to display historical data (as a heat map) and recent data (as interactive tips) as a final undergraduate course requirement. Safely app aims to preemptively inform users of dangerous situations before they enter the physical proximity of danger with real-time data.


DEVELOPERS: Justine Edrozo, Kevin Ke
DATA ANALYST: Brooks Lobe

The problem and design challenge.

Being in a large city, Seattle is home to a lot of crime. Currently, there is no reliable way for someone to know whether an area is safe without going in-person and investigating. Our goal for the project was to utilize data from local authorities and the community to highlight danger in real-time with a mobile app.


GOALS AND OUTCOMES

- Design and build a mobile app that helps the user find and walk the safest route home.

- Provide real-time updates of “unsafe” areas via crowdsourced posting on an interactive map.

- Provide an interface for recent and archival crime reports for the city of Seattle.

UNIVERSITY OF WASHINGTON INFORMATICS CAPSTONE

The primary goal of the 2-quarter long Capstone class project was to demonstrate the knowledge gained within the Informatics program to identify and define a real-world information problem by integrating people, technology, and information to make a positive social impact for our community.

Each team was to develop the approach and methods needed to address the problem, conduct the research and present the findings in both oral and written formats. As a final deliverable, teams presented their projects to faculty, family, colleagues, and sponsors in a Capstone event at the end of the school year.

OUR APPROACH AND PROCESS OVERVIEW

Having a strong UX design background as a team allowed us to follow a lean approach in order to design, build and test our product in a timely manner.

We spent the first 10-weeks conducting research through user surveys, producing a competitive analysis, and creating storyboards. Additionally, we created a comprehensive design blueprint consisting of a mood board, wireframes, prototypes, and design specifications. The remaining 10-weeks consisted of user testing and iOS app development.

MY ROLE

I was responsible for the experience strategy and design of the iOS application. I primarily led our team through the complete UI design process while assisting with related UX works.

Discovering our audience with user research.

WHAT DOES "SAFETY" MEAN TO YOU?

We designed and sent out a Google survey to various family, friends, and coworkers via convenience sampling to gain a better understanding of our users’ safety habits. Some participants were also briefly interviewed afterward to elaborate on some of their responses.


We wanted to know more about how our users perceived their safety living in Seattle and to learn more about what current devices or strategies they used to protect themselves while commuting. We had 50 individuals respond to the survey, 90% of which were in the age range of 19-24 and 10% in the age range of 13-18. A combination of quantitative and qualitative questions were asked. After coding and analyzing our results, we came up with the following conclusions that validated and provided insight for some of our initial assumptions:

Users feel less safe when walking alone, in the dark, or in unfamiliar areas.

This was an assumption that our team initially made at the start of our project and were not surprised by the nearly unanimous response set we received after asking our users to describe what circumstances they felt less/safer. Several participants also commented feeling safer when they were walking with a male friend. If we have the time and resources, our team may consider adding additional support or features specific to female users. Some interviewed participants also commented that they consciously made an effort not to use their phone or take it out of their pockets while walking (for fear of becoming an easy target for robbery or assault).

“Safety” means both physical and mental well-being.

When asked “What does safety mean to you?” we received very similar responses related to the users’ desire to be physically out of harm’s way and also having peace of mind and not having to worry about being harmed, targeted, or assaulted. Users used phrases like “feeling confident, not worried”, “not having to question my personal well-being”, “absence of fear”, and “not having to worry about being harmed”.

Young adults DO care about their safety.

When asked on a scale from 1 to 5 (1=not important at all and 5=extremely important), 80% of participants responded 5 and 20% of participants responded 4. 38% of the participants felt like Seattle was a relatively safe city in comparison to other cities in the country and about half the participants responded they sometimes walked by themselves and sometimes with other people.



The top 3 most used emergency/safety services used by our participants were UW Alerts, calling a friend, and messaging a friend. Clearly, our users value staying informed and being up-to-date about their surroundings, which validates our project’s existence. Although we originally tabled the idea to include a more social aspect of the app (i.e., chat), we may need to consider some changes to our UI and our user flows because our users heavily rely on their friend/social groups for their safety.

UW Alerts is highly subscribed to, however, many participants felt like it is underutilized and often too vague.



About 50% of our participants used UW Alerts, 40% relied on friends and word-of-mouth, and the remaining 10% relied on other means of learning about crime or emergency situations, like social media. Interviewing our participants about UW Alerts validated some of our previous assumptions about their frustrations with the current system. Many participants mentioned only receiving ‘vague’ statements from UWPD hours or days after incidents, or not receiving alerts at all about “smaller” incidents like assault, robbery, or petty theft. None of our participants responded hearing about an incident as it was happening, and only 16% said they heard about it minutes after. Despite this, many of our interviewed participants expressed a desire for knowing this information in real-time.

UNDERSTANDING OUR TARGETED DEMOGRAPHICS

Based on our Google survey data, we were able to focus on who we are designing for along with who we are not designing for by designating persona types that carefully aligned with our product vision. Originally, our targeted users evolved around commuters who rely on walking or biking as their primary mode of transportation. We soon realized that our product solution may go a step further and target Seattle tourists and new Seattle community members who are looking for a new home within a safe enviornment.

The creative process.

THINKING BEHIND THE USER INTERFACE

While it is common to put together a mood board illustrating UI elements, I instead began with creating a user experience mood board that focused on various approaches to information architecture, user support, usability engineering, and interaction design for mobile devices.

DESIGNING FOR FAMILIARITY

During the early stages of brainstorming and ideation, we spent some time understanding what makes apps like Uber, Snapchat, and Google Maps easy to use and navigate for first time users. We discovered that each of their primary function is presented to users almost immediately after the app opens, giving users an instant call to action.


We decided to follow a similar concept by having a map view be the first thing a user sees when the Safely app opens. Doing this, 1) emphasizes Seattle crime data at first glance, 2) demonstrates how safe a user is at their current location, 3) allows users to take the precaution of how safe the areas leading to their destination will be.

Data that protects.

CRIME HISTORY

Safely users can quickly view historical data about crime in Seattle that is displayed as a thermal map. Grabbing data from Seattle Police Department's (UWPD included) API gave us the ability to integrate information in a way that gives users a geographic sense of where the most “dangerous” areas are nearby or in neighborhoods of interest.

REAL-TIME INCIDENTS

Safely users can view and interact with real-time community-submitted incidents about unsafe areas that have occurred in close proximity (or distant) prior to leaving for their destination. This simply acts as a high-level neighborhood watch where a single user can make a social impact to the Seattle community.

ROUTE OVERVIEW

Originally, we envisioned the Safely app guiding users to their destinations on a turn-by-turn basis but as much as we wanted to get our users from point A to point B, we needed to think about why users would want to use our navigation system over current leading solutions such as Google Maps. Eventually, user testing results showed that people would much rather prefer using Google and Apple Maps to get to their destination.


So we simplified the turn-by-turn navigational system by giving users the ability to set an end destination that displays routes with varying safety ranges in an aerial map view. Once a route is selected, the aerial map view remains and follows the user's current location until they safely reach their destination. This gives users the freedom to use their preferred navigational app while Safely becomes more of a precautionary step to take in foreseeing danger before starting a trip.

USERS HELPING OTHERS

Safely users are informed in real-time through push notifications when a crime is posted nearby or in areas along a route. As a primary feature of the Safely app, those who use it can submit an incident to warn others about a dangerous situation or disturbance they have observed or experienced. Submitted incidents are tagged and saved for 72 hours as an interactive marker on the map that specifies the type of crime encountered along with a detailed description.

PINPOINTING CRIME IN EMERGENCY SITUATIONS

Informational interview results gave us reason to rethink our approach on how users inform the Safely community, especially during emergency situations. Originally, we designed Safely to work in a way that gives users the ability to inform others only at the moment an incident occurs. Problem is, that is not always the case.

In reality, at the time of an emergency situation, users will prioritize their personal safety over the safety of others. They will not take the time to pause during a high concentrated situation to inform others but will rather do so at a time when they feel safe. With that in mind, we redesigned the way users contribute to the Safely app by giving them the ability to quickly drop a marker (location of incident) during their route which can then be given more detail once they have safely reached their destination.

Designing the user interface.

THE PROCESS

I started the UI design process by creating wireframes in AdobeXD that demonstrated an adopted Snapchat-like information architecture that we were able to work from as an evolving UI. We then began to dedicate each screen to represent feature capabilities that included, 1) crowdsourced/SPD incident markers plotted on a map, 2) a search route menu, 3) a timeline feed of nearby incidents, 4) suggested route overview, 5) en-route display.


Once we defined core user functionalities, it then became my responsibility to simplify, design, and iterate each feature through user testing until results showed measures of intuitive interactions when dealing with the data (incident markers/thermal map) and when fewer questions were asked during prototype testing. Because of time constraints, early design considerations that enabled users to get in touch with emergency contacts and authorities were dropped, allowing our developers to focus on geofencing alerts and notifications.


The challenge in creating a usable and visually appealing interface was to make our collected data understandable at a glance. I followed minimal design principles to create an interface that emphasized the data and its source by utilizing color, iconography, button placement, and user status.

The UI Evolution: A look at how the user interface came to be.

Detailed specifications.

INTERACTION DESIGN TEST

A critical part of designing the user interface over an iterative process was to ensure that it was usable while designing to keep it minimal. I understood that visual communication can be broken up into three organizational attributes that demonstrate relationships among elements, in this case, UI elements.


HIERARCHY = a dominant-subordinate relationship among elements

GROUPING = a relationship that specifies the elements to be associated together

SEQUENCE = a relationship among elements that specifies first, second, third...


With this in mind, I decided to create a grayscale prototype for usability testing in order to discover if the position, size, shape, orientation, alignment, and proximity of UI elements spoke for themselves as actionable items. With the removal of color, I was able to observe how users interacted with the UI's information architecture. Learning about what areas needed user experience improvement helped create a smooth transition into designing a high-fidelity prototype that emphasized color as dominate element of hierarchy.

UI ASSETS

I used color to distinguish the source of data received from users (red) and from the Seattle Police Department (teal). Once we determined that user data would be represented as incident markers plotted on a map, I used icons from the Noun Project to specify the type of crime for each incident.

The experience design prototype.

Emphasizing emotional design.

VISCERAL EMOTIONAL DESIGN
This type of emotional design is what motivated me to take on the role as the UI designer from the start of the project. I wanted to challenge myself to create a quality experience that looked and felt easy to use for first-time users by emphasizing concepts of minimalism. Successfully doing so would highlight the importance of Safely's data and core functionalities.


BEHAVIORAL EMOTIONAL DESIGN
As much as I wanted to design a visually appealing app, I also knew it was important to create a functional user experience. At the core, Safely is designed to protect people from unsafe situations with real-time crime data, meaning that it was important to establish a sense of security during initial interactions. Having that as a key concept allowed me to really evaluate how well and intuitive each functionality performs before transferring my designs over to our developers.


REFLECTIVE EMOTIONAL DESIGN
At the end of the day, reflective emotional design is what matters the most to Safely (and to most digital solutions). Once users find that our product has the potential to make an impact on their lives, we hope it keeps them coming back for more because it holds intrinsic value. Although it was critical to design our Safely app to look appealing and be usable, no value would be given to our product if users did not feel the need to use it for their protection.
